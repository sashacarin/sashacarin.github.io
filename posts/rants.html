The "s" in sci-fi stands for sexist.

I dont know what the rest of the letters stand for, but thats probably what the s stands for. I've been thinking a lot about this lately, not because I deeply felt the effects of sexism in science fiction, but because I wonder what its effects have been.

Its a common theme in sci-fi to hypothysize what "alien science" might be like. Even just recently, Hail Mary had its big moment with the Gosling movie coming out, and everyone deciding that NASA is cool again. The whole reason the story is fun is because a human gets to figure out how to communicate with an alien by using math and science, the universal expressions supersetting language. But we dont know, we dont really know that a different form of intellegence would comprehend thoses invariant physiscal properties in the way that we do. Imagine an intellegence that was specificaly good at comprehending things in 4 dimensions, where lots math and topology is completely wacky to us. Or what would the scientific method mean to something thinking in parrallel processes?

Theres more here, something for another post though. The real question is; Does it matter that there's no chicks in Sci-Fi?

The answer is obviously yes, if only for the matter of the inspiration of future generations of scientists. But aside from the general injustices and waste of perfectly good brains, the question persists, Is the Female experience so "alien" to the Male as to cause a divergence in our current conception of science?

I tenatively answer yes ... Not for a difference in genetics, or phsyical (mental) ability, but because of culture. We can see this best when we look at the evolution of "Scientific Culture", how knowledge first came from wise men, and religous leaders, then philosopers and inventors, before slowly bluring into "scientists", and now becoming closer and closer to technicians. Each moment in scientific history has had its own culture, and these have diretly impacted the problems they chose to think about, the manner in which they persued them, and the bredth (or narrowness) of their expertiese. Arguably the greatest geniuses in history came out of a culture much topicaly brouder than our modern one. But then, and even now, sci-fi and science cuture is still very "male". I think thats a loss.
