Irrepresentability

2026-06-23

0. Standing conventions

We reason in an informal metatheory (ZFC). To avoid proper-class pathologies we fix once and for all a set $G$, the global domain of all things under discussion, rather than a proper class (Remark 7). All cardinal arithmetic is metatheoretic. “$\aleph_0$” denotes countable infinity; “at most countable” means finite or countably infinite.

1. Definitions

Definition 1.1 (Formal language). A formal language is a pair $(\Sigma, L)$ where the alphabet $\Sigma$ is an at most countable set of symbols and $L \subseteq \Sigma^{*}$ is a set of finite strings over $\Sigma$ (the well-formed expressions). Since $\Sigma$ is at most countable, so is $\Sigma^{*}$, hence $|L| \le \aleph_0$.

Definition 1.2 (Representation system). A representation system over $G$ is a triple $\mathcal{S} = (\Sigma, L, \rho)$ where $(\Sigma, L)$ is a formal language and the denotation $\rho \colon L \rightharpoonup G$ is a partial function. We read $\rho(w) = x$ as “the expression $w$ represents the thing $x$.” Functionality encodes that a fixed expression, in a fixed system, picks out at most one thing; ambiguity is handled in Remark 1.

Definition 1.3 (The representable and the irrepresentable). Fix an at most countable family of representation systems $\mathcal{R} = \{\mathcal{S}_i\}_{i \in I}$, $|I| \le \aleph_0$, $\mathcal{S}_i = (\Sigma_i, L_i, \rho_i)$. Define

\[ O_f \;:=\; \bigcup_{i \in I} \operatorname{ran}(\rho_i) \;=\; \{\, x \in G : \exists\, i\in I,\ \exists\, w \in L_i,\ \rho_i(w)=x \,\}, \qquad O_n \;:=\; G \setminus O_f . \]

$O_f$ is the class of formally representable things, $O_n$ its complement in $G$, and $G = O_f \sqcup O_n$ is a partition.

Assumption A (the bridge: Verbalizable $\iff$ Formally Representable). A thing is verbalizable iff it lies in $O_f$ for the family $\mathcal{R}$ comprising the formalizable fragments of natural language. Assumption A is what licenses transporting any conclusion about $O_f / O_n$ into a conclusion about what can and cannot be said. It is a premise (you may call it an axiom), not a theorem; its plausibility — and in particular the rule-following tension raised in Q2 — is treated in §6. The honest reading is extensional: A concerns finished utterances, not the open-ended act of meaning-making.

Terminology note (information $:=$ $O_f$). We deliberately speak of “things,” not “information.” In Shannon and Kolmogorov theory “information” is formal by construction (bits, codewords, programs), so naming the elements of $G$ “information” would pre-decide $O_n = \emptyset$ and beg the question. We therefore define

\[ \text{information} \;:=\; O_f \quad(\text{the finitely codeable part of }G), \]

so the entire program is the slogan things $\supsetneq$ information: $O_n$ is the non-informational — what there is but cannot be coded. (See Q7.)

2. The counting lemma

Lemma 2.1 (Representability is countable). $|O_f| \le \aleph_0$.

Proof. For each $i$, $L_i$ is at most countable (Def. 1.1) and $\rho_i$ is a partial function, so $|\operatorname{ran}(\rho_i)| \le |L_i| \le \aleph_0$. Then $O_f$ is a union indexed by the at most countable $I$ of at most countable sets; by the countable union theorem (countable choice suffices), $|O_f| \le \aleph_0 \cdot \aleph_0 = \aleph_0$. ∎

Remark 1 (ambiguity does not help). If denotation is relational — one expression $w$ admitting a set $\rho_i[w] \subseteq G$ of referents — the lemma survives provided each $\rho_i[w]$ is at most countable, since $O_f$ is then still a countable union of countable sets. It fails only if a single finite expression may denote uncountably many things at once; but such a system represents nothing in particular and trivializes Assumption A. Countable denotation is the principled setting.

Remark 2 (description-length / compression form). Define the description length $\ell(x) := \min\{\,|w| : \rho_i(w)=x \text{ for some } i\,\}$, with $\ell(x)=\infty$ when $x$ has no representative; equivalently $\ell$ is a Kolmogorov complexity relative to $\mathcal{R}$. Then $O_f = \{x : \ell(x) < \infty\}$. For each $n$ there are at most $\sum_{k \le n}\sum_i |\Sigma_i|^{k}$ expressions of length $\le n$, a finite number per fixed finite alphabet (and countable overall), so $\{x : \ell(x) \le n\}$ is at most countable and $O_f = \bigcup_{n} \{x : \ell(x) \le n\}$ is countable. This re-proves Lemma 2.1 in the language of compression: to represent is to compress to a finite codeword, and only countably many finite codewords exist.

3. The cardinality proposition (the corrected “Corollary”)

The draft labelled this a Corollary and wrote the half-formed “$|O_f| >$”. It is not a corollary of anything prior; the intended content is the existence claim below.

Proposition 3.1. If $|G| > \aleph_0$, then $O_n \neq \emptyset$; more precisely $|O_n| = |G|$ while $|O_f| \le \aleph_0$. Both $O_f$ and $O_n$ are proper and nonempty as soon as $G$ contains at least one representable thing.

Proof. By Lemma 2.1, $|O_f| \le \aleph_0 < |G|$. If $O_n$ were at most countable then $|G| = |O_f \cup O_n| \le \aleph_0 + \aleph_0 = \aleph_0$, a contradiction; so $|O_n| > \aleph_0$, hence $O_n \ne \emptyset$. Moreover $|O_n| \le |G| = |O_f \cup O_n| \le |O_f| + |O_n| = |O_n|$ (the last step since $|O_n|$ is infinite and absorbs $|O_f| \le \aleph_0$), so $|O_n| = |G|$ by Cantor–Schröder–Bernstein. $O_f \neq \emptyset$ exactly when some $\rho_i$ is nonempty. ∎

Proposition 3.2 (the stochastic case is the same case). Let representation be stochastic in any of the usual senses — (i) a randomized generator/recognizer; (ii) a probability assignment over expressions (e.g. a Solomonoff/algorithmic prior $M(x)=\sum_{p:\,U(p)=x}2^{-|p|}$); or (iii) things represented by probability distributions. Then still $|O_f| \le \aleph_0$, hence $O_n \neq \emptyset$ whenever $|G| > \aleph_0$.

Proof. (i) The strings a randomized machine can emit with positive probability, ranging over all seeds, form a subset of $\Sigma^{*}$, hence countable. (ii) A prior is a probability measure over the countable set of expressions, so its support — the positively-weighted representations — is at most countable. (iii) There are $2^{\aleph_0}$ distributions, but only the finitely specifiable (computable) ones are usable finite representations, and those are countable; an arbitrary real-parametrised distribution has no finite description and is itself a member of $O_n$, so option (iii) relocates irrepresentability without removing it. In each case the representable set injects into a countable set of finite specifications, so Lemma 2.1 applies unchanged. ∎

So the draft's deterministic/stochastic split is unnecessary: the count is blind to a system's internal mechanism because it only ever counts finite expressions.

Remark 3 (why Gödel is the wrong tool, and what it does give). The draft's Case 1 invoked incompleteness to obtain $O_n \neq \emptyset$. This does not go through. Gödel's first theorem: every consistent, recursively axiomatised $T \supseteq Q$ (Robinson arithmetic) admits a sentence $G_T$ true in $\mathbb{N}$ but unprovable in $T$. But $G_T$ is a well-formed formula — a finite string, hence representable ($G_T \in O_f$); what it is not is $T$-provable. Incompleteness separates truth from provability, not things from their representations; Tarski's undefinability likewise concerns a system's internal reach, not the size of $O_f$. The incompleteness phenomena yield at most:

(Secondary route.) No single consistent r.e. $T \supseteq Q$ decides every truth in its own language.

This is a claim about deductive capture, which neither entails nor is entailed by the representability claim $O_n \neq \emptyset$: the two are logically independent, and incompleteness simply does not reach the conclusion the draft wanted from it. The cardinality count is what delivers irrepresentability.

4. Theorem (human experience)

Let $E_h \subseteq G$, $E_h \neq \emptyset$, be the class of things experienced by humans. (We use non-strict $\subseteq$: properness is never invoked — see Q5.)

Theorem 4.1. If $|E_h| > \aleph_0$, then $E_h \cap O_n \neq \emptyset$ (indeed $|E_h \cap O_n| = |E_h|$). Equivalently, under Assumption A: if the space of human experience is uncountable, some experience is unverbalizable.

Proof. Suppose $E_h \cap O_n = \emptyset$. Then $E_h \subseteq O_f$. Compose the inclusion $E_h \hookrightarrow O_f$ with the choice $O_f \to \bigsqcup_i L_i,\ x \mapsto (i,w)$ for some $w$ with $\rho_i(w)=x$; this is an injection of $E_h$ into the at most countable set $\bigsqcup_i L_i$ (injective because $\rho_i$ is a function, so a codeword determines its referent). Hence $|E_h| \le \aleph_0$, contradicting $|E_h| > \aleph_0$. Therefore $E_h \cap O_n \neq \emptyset$. The refinement repeats the split of Prop. 3.1 with $E_h$ for $G$: $|E_h| \le |E_h\cap O_f| + |E_h\cap O_n| \le \aleph_0 + |E_h\cap O_n|$ forces $|E_h \cap O_n| = |E_h|$. ∎

Remark 4 (resolving the draft's placeholders). The draft groped for “$f \colon O_f \to E_h$ (probably wrong?)” then “$f \colon G \to E_h$ (maybe right?)”. Neither: the needed morphism runs the other way and is an injection $E_h \hookrightarrow \bigsqcup_i L_i$; the contradiction is that an uncountable set cannot inject into a countable one. No surjection onto $E_h$, no “compression function,” and no appeal to the Proposition are required — Lemma 2.1 alone closes it. The draft's unease about $f\colon O_f \to E_h$ was right: that arrow is reversed.

Remark 5 (the theorem is exactly as strong as its premise). Theorem 4.1 is conditional, honestly so. The machinery manufactures nothing; it converts the target into one load-bearing premise

\[ (\dagger)\qquad |E_h| > \aleph_0 . \]

For: experiential qualities seem to vary along continua (pitch, brightness, hue, pain intensity); if distinct points of a quality continuum are distinct experiences, $|E_h| \ge 2^{\aleph_0}$. Against: perception may be quantised by finite just-noticeable-difference thresholds and bounded neural state, making the discriminable experiences finite, hence $\le \aleph_0$ and the theorem silent. This is an empirical/philosophical question; the formalization's value is to locate it exactly. (See Q6 for how the choice of formalization of $E_h$ is the same act as asserting or denying $(\dagger)$.)

Remark 6 (compression form, and lossless vs. lossy). Read through Remark 2: $x$ is losslessly representable iff $\ell(x) < \infty$ iff $x \in O_f$. Theorem 4.1 then says: if $|E_h| > \aleph_0$, the coding map $c \colon E_h \to \{0,1\}^{*}$ cannot be total and injective — you cannot losslessly compress uncountably many experiences into finite codewords — so some $x \in E_h$ has $\ell(x) = \infty$: an incompressible experience, admitting no finite exact description. That is the ineffable element, and it is exactly the “compression function” the draft reached for, with the arrow corrected. The lossy counterpart sharpens the picture: language describes approximately, and rate–distortion theory gives, for a continuous source, $R(D) < \infty$ for every tolerance $D > 0$ but $R(D) \to \infty$ as $D \to 0$. One can always say something (finite bits, $D>0$) yet never say it exactly ($D=0$ needs infinite bits). A countable dense codebook approximates a separable $E_h$ to any $\delta > 0$; exact capture of uncountably many points is impossible. “Words approximate the sunset but never equal it” becomes the statement $R(0) = \infty$. (Full development in Q8.)

5. Foundational caveats

Remark 7 (set vs. proper class). If “all things” is a proper class, “$|G|$” is not a cardinal and Prop. 3.1 is ill-typed. The §0 fix presumes the totality can be set-bounded; it suffices throughout that $G$ merely contain an uncountable set (a copy of $\mathbb{R}$, read as magnitudes among “things”), to which the arguments localize.

Remark 8 (Richard, Berry, pointwise-definable models). Lemma 2.1 counts expressions under an externally fixed denotation, so $O_f$ is a genuine set of size $\le \aleph_0$ and the conclusion is immune to Richard's and Berry's paradoxes — which arise only when “is representable” is internalised as an object-language predicate (Tarski forbids this). One subtlety: there are models of ZFC in which every set is parameter-free definable (pointwise-definable models, Hamkins–Linetsky–Reitz); there the internal analogue of $O_f$ exhausts the universe and internal $O_n = \emptyset$. This does not contradict Prop. 3.1 (a statement about an externally fixed countable $\mathcal{R}$), but it shows “$O_n = \emptyset$?” is sensitive to the internal/external reading and to which $\mathcal{R}$ is admitted. Assumption A must fix $\mathcal{R}$ externally.

6. Questions, problems, and the lot

Q1. Does Gödel's contradiction criterion allow the creation of new information in the deterministic case? How would one prove it?

Make “new information” precise; the hunch is half-right, and which half depends on the sense.

Deductive sense (independent sentences). Yes. By the second incompleteness theorem a consistent r.e. $T \supseteq \mathrm{PA}$ does not prove $\mathrm{Con}(T)$, so $T_1 := T + \mathrm{Con}(T)$ proves a sentence $T$ could not; iterating, $T_{n+1} := T_n + \mathrm{Con}(T_n)$ — and the transfinite progressions of Turing (1939) and Feferman (1962) — keep producing sentences independent of every earlier stage. Along suitable paths such progressions prove all true $\Pi^0_1$ sentences. So new theorems genuinely appear. But the deterministic system does not generate them autonomously: each extension requires adding an axiom (choosing $G_T$, or $\mathrm{Con}(T_n)$, or a path through ordinal notations), and at the transfinite stages selecting the path imports information contained in no $T_n$. The “creation” is really an external choice made at the gap incompleteness leaves open — which is exactly your intuition that the contradiction criterion (proving $G_T$ would breach consistency, so $G_T$ is left undecided) opens a slot where new information can be inserted.

Algorithmic sense (Kolmogorov complexity). No — and this is the clean theorem to prove the hunch against. Chaitin's incompleteness theorem: for a consistent r.e. theory $T$ there is a constant $c \approx K(T) + O(1)$ such that $T$ proves no statement of the form “$K(s) > c$,” although all but finitely many strings $s$ satisfy it. A formal system cannot certify information beyond roughly its own Kolmogorov complexity. So a fixed deterministic system cannot spontaneously create unbounded new (certified) information; it is capped by its own size.

Tie to the framework. Either way nothing escapes $O_f$: every $G_T$, every $\mathrm{Con}(T_n)$, is a finite string, hence representable. “New information” in the deductive sense is movement within $O_f$ — which representable truths get certified — never a crossing into $O_n$. The cardinality wall is untouched. How to prove your version: fix the sense, then cite (deductive) the 2nd incompleteness theorem + Feferman progressions for strict gain, or (algorithmic) Chaitin's bound for the ceiling.

Q2. Language follows rules, yet was created into rule-following. How does that gel with Assumption A?

The tension is real and is the Wittgenstein/Kripkenstein point: natural language is an open, self-extending practice; its “rules” are descriptive and revisable, meaning is use, and no finite statement of a rule fixes the rule (the rule-following regress). Resolve it by splitting two things A could be about:

  • Products — finished utterances/expressions. Even an ever-extending language coins only countably many finite expressions across countably many stages, so Lemma 2.1 still bounds the products: open-endedness does not escape $\aleph_0$. A is safe here.
  • The generative act — the instituting and altering of meaning. No finite rule fixes it (regress), so the meaning-conferring practice is plausibly itself in $O_n$.

So read A extensionally (about what gets said): it survives, and powers the theorems. Read intensionally (about the saying), it is doubtful — and that doubt is not a problem but evidence for the program: the very capacity that creates language looks like an $O_n$ resident. Net recommendation: state A extensionally, and note that the generativity of language is itself a candidate ineffable.

Q3. Doesn't the “corollary” imply $O_f$ is empty?

No. It implies $|O_f| \le \aleph_0$ and $|O_n| = |G|$: $O_f$ is countable, not empty. “Almost all things lie in $O_n$” is not “$O_f$ is empty” — a countably infinite set is vanishingly small beside an uncountable one yet still richly populated. For any non-degenerate language $O_f \neq \emptyset$: it contains $0,1,2,\dots$, every finite string, every nameable object — countably infinitely many. Only the degenerate empty/everywhere-undefined $\mathcal{R}$ gives $O_f = \emptyset$. The worry likely comes from the discarded sub-case (“a contradiction forces removing elements from $O_f$”); under the cardinality argument there is no such erosion. $O_f$ is small but populous.

Q4. How is the stochastic case of the “corollary” done?

It is now Proposition 3.2 in the body, with proof. In brief, three readings of “stochastic formalization” all give $|O_f| \le \aleph_0$: (i) a randomized machine's positive-probability outputs are still finite strings (countable); (ii) an algorithmic/Solomonoff prior is supported on the computable strings (countable); (iii) representing things by distributions admits $2^{\aleph_0}$ distributions, but only the finitely specifiable ones are usable representations (countable), and the rest are themselves in $O_n$ — stochasticity relocates irrepresentability, never removes it. Hence $O_n \neq \emptyset$ when $|G| > \aleph_0$, exactly as in the deterministic case. The upshot worth keeping: there was never a separate stochastic burden, because the count sees only finite specifications.

Q5. In the Theorem I wrote $E_h \subsetneq G$ (proper). Does it matter? Would $\subseteq$ imply human experience could be of all things?

It does not matter to the theorem: the proof uses only $E_h \neq \emptyset$ and $|E_h| > \aleph_0$; properness is never invoked. (I switched to $\subseteq$ for the weakest hypothesis.) On the logic: $\subseteq$ permits $E_h = G$ but does not imply it — non-strict inclusion allows equality without asserting it, so it does not claim humans experience everything. $\subsetneq$ asserts $G \setminus E_h \neq \emptyset$ (some thing goes unexperienced) — independently plausible but unused here; indeed even if $E_h = G$ the theorem holds trivially, since then $O_n \subseteq G = E_h$ gives $E_h \cap O_n = O_n \neq \emptyset$. Use $\subseteq$ for generality; assert $\subsetneq$ only if you separately want “humans don't experience everything,” which the argument does not need.

Q6. Can we formalize the definition of $E_h$?

Yes — as a set — and doing so is consistent with the theorem, because naming a set is not representing its members. “$\mathbb{R}$” finitely names the reals though almost every real is undefinable; likewise a finite definition of $E_h$ does not drop its elements into $O_f$. The catch: the choice of formalization decides the premise $(\dagger)$.

  • A functional/finite-state identification (an experience $=$ a brain or computational state) makes $E_h$ finite or countable, so $(\dagger)$ fails and Theorem 4.1 is vacuous.
  • A quality-space model — $E_h \subseteq Q = \prod_{j} I_j$, each $I_j$ a continuum (hue, brightness, pitch, intensity, …), experiences taken modulo indistinguishability — gives $|E_h| = 2^{\aleph_0}$, so $(\dagger)$ holds and the theorem bites.

Recommended: define $E_h$ as a nonempty subset of such a quality space — finitely named, uncountably populated. This is the formalization most faithful to the project, and it makes explicit that to formalize $E_h$ this way and to assert $(\dagger)$ are the same act. So: formalizable as a set, yes; just don't expect that formalization to enumerate experiences — enumeration is precisely what $O_n$ forbids.

Q7. “Things” vs. “information”: is there a better adaptation?

Your instinct is correct and worth keeping. In information theory “information” is formal by construction (it is bits/codewords/programs), so labelling the elements of $G$ “information” smuggles formalizability into the domain and makes $O_n = \emptyset$ by fiat — question begged. The clean fix is the Terminology note in §1: keep “things” for $G$ and define information $:= O_f$, the finitely codeable part. Then the thesis is the slogan things $\supsetneq$ information, and $O_n$ is “the non-informational.” This dissolves the worry: information is, correctly, the formal subset; the theorem says the world of things strictly exceeds it. Nearest existing vocabulary: Floridi's data → information hierarchy (data as primitive, pre-meaning differences) is the closest, but even “data” leans formal; “things”/“entities” stays maximally neutral. Keep “things,” and let “information” be the derived, bounded notion.

Q8. How to finish the Theorem along the compression track?

This is now carried by Remark 2 (definitions) and Remark 6 (the theorem); here is the whole arc.

Representation $=$ compression. Set $\ell(x)$ $=$ length of the shortest expression denoting $x$ ($=\infty$ if none); equivalently a Kolmogorov complexity $K(x)$ relative to a universal machine. Then $O_f = \{x : \ell(x) < \infty\}$: the representable things are exactly the finitely compressible ones.

The count, re-derived. Only finitely many things have $\ell \le n$ per fixed alphabet, so $O_f = \bigcup_n \{\ell \le n\}$ is countable (Lemma 2.1, compression form).

The theorem, finished. If $|E_h| > \aleph_0$, the coding map $c \colon E_h \to \{0,1\}^{*}$ cannot be total and injective — uncountably many experiences cannot be losslessly compressed into finite codewords — so some $x \in E_h$ has $\ell(x) = K(x) = \infty$: an incompressible experience, no finite exact description. That element is the ineffable one. This is your “compression function,” arrow corrected: $c$ lives on $O_f$ and cannot extend over all of $E_h$.

The interesting track, made precise — lossless vs. lossy. Lossless (exact) representability is $\ell(x) < \infty$, i.e. $x \in O_f$; uncountable $E_h$ $\Rightarrow$ most experiences are not losslessly representable. Language, though, compresses lossily — “the sunset was beautiful” is a low-rate approximation. Rate–distortion theory: for a continuous source, $R(D) < \infty$ for every tolerance $D > 0$ but $R(D) \to \infty$ as $D \to 0$ (e.g. Gaussian $R(D) = \tfrac12\log(\sigma^2/D)$). So you can always say something (finite bits, $D > 0$) yet never say it exactly ($D = 0$ demands infinite bits). A countable dense codebook approximates a separable $E_h$ to any $\delta > 0$; exact capture of uncountably many points is impossible. Ineffability lives in the rate–distortion residual — the part every finite (lossy) description discards. “Words approximate the experience but never equal it” is then the theorem $R(0) = \infty$.
